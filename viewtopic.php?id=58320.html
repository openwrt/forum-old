Hey there.

I don't think having things like "59% have no problems with device xyz, 12% can't use 5GHz, 29% had no luck at all" as a memers rating summary is usefull for anybody.

Instead, having a single table of hardware that allows to compare different devices against each other in terms of specific features without that "percentage of: works well" is the way to go. And that's the way zo0oks thread goes.

I'm not sure if that shure if that should target the direction of recommending a speciic device. Just look at the current TP-Link hype devices, C5 and C7. At the moment, I wouldn't go for anything else. The C5 is awesom in terms of price/performance ratio. But just as former products, I think in a couple of month from now they are not sold any longer but have reacht their end of life. Then there will be other products to recommend and a recommendation made today is useless.

And that's what's going to happen if a huge number of different devices is recommended by a relatively small number of individuals each.

Instead, one must somehow "downrate" a specific member vote depending on its age and make more recent votes count more than older ones. And out of a sudden, you're guessing "magic numbers" in a statistical problem.

There's a good chance that costs days and days of implementation and adjustment and in the end causes more problems and confusion than if there was no such a thing at all.

Regards,
Stephan.

At this point there is a second problem.  Routers go out of production so quickly that by the time a released version of openwrt supports it, it is either out of production already or within a few months of being out of production.   

My favorite router, the Netgear 3700v4 seemed so promising.  It had 128 MByte of dram and 128MByte of flash and had all the other normal specs with 2 and 5 ghz radio and 5 1-gigabit ethernet connections.   I recommended it to all my friends looking to run openwrt on modern hardware.   By the time BB 14.07 came out, the router already had  one foot in the grave.  What we really need is a commitment from router manufacturers to 1) clearly mark the router packages with the hardware version and more importantly 2) make the hardware of similar model numbers upward compatible.
